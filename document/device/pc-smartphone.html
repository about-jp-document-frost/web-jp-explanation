1. PCs preferences


When people use a computer outside of work, they usually choose:

  • Low‑end consumer laptops
  • Entry‑level Windows machines from domestic brands (NEC, Fujitsu, Dynabook)
  • Many students use MacBooks for schoolwork and creative tasks

The average user doesn't customize or optimize their system.

They just buy whatever is sold in big electronics stores.

2. Smartphones preferences


Japan is a very smartphone‑centric country.

And when it comes to smartphones, most Japanese people choose iPhones.

This is not because of technical reasons, but because of:

  • Brand image and social status
  • Easy data transfer when upgrading
  • A huge market for phone decorations and accessories
  • Peer pressure(同調圧力, dōchō atsuryoku) — "everyone else uses iPhone, so I also use same one to have same experience (some students get bullied if they don't. We say this social pressure, "同調圧力(dōchō atsuryoku, peer pressure)")"

So the overall consumer tech culture is simple, convenient, and trend‑driven, not performance‑driven.

3. In companies, the standard is “safe and boring” Windows laptops

In the workplace, Japanese companies overwhelmingly use:

  • Windows laptops
  • Usually from NEC, Fujitsu, HP, Dell, Lenovo
  • Very conservative specs
  • Strict IT management
  • Heavy security software
  • No admin rights for employees

The priority is stability and compliance, not performance.

4. Gaming or high‑performance PCs are extremely niche

Unlike the US or Europe, Japan has:

  • Very few desktop PC users
  • Very few custom‑build enthusiasts
  • Very few people using high‑end GPUs

Most Japanese gamers play on:

  • Nintendo Switch
  • PlayStation
  • Mobile games

PC gaming culture is small compared to the West.

5. My own case is considered unusual in Japan

I personally use:

  • Android smartphones (rare in Japan)
  • High‑performance devices like the ASUS ROG Phone
  • Non‑Japanese keyboard layouts
  • Customizable, high‑spec PCs

In Japan, this often surprises people.

Some even ask whether I’m really Japanese because my device choices don’t match the local stereotype.

But globally, my setup is completely normal—especially among engineers.

6. Summary

If I summarize Japan in one sentence:

Japan uses computers, but it is not a PC‑culture country.

Smartphones dominate, and workplaces rely on conservative Windows laptops.

People like me—who use high‑spec devices, Android phones, and custom setups—are a minority here, even though it’s standard in Europe or the US.
